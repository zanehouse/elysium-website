Regime Inflection: Why the Disinflation Trade May Be Exhausted

February 2025 Elysian Capital Research ~12 min read

Overview

For over a decade following the Global Financial Crisis, the structural case for long-duration assets was compelling: secular stagnation, demographic headwinds, and a credibly disinflationary Federal Reserve combined to anchor real rates at historically low levels. That regime, while interrupted in 2022, appears to be approaching a more durable transition.

This essay examines the confluence of leading indicators that suggest the disinflation trade — the reflexive bid for duration in risk-off environments — may no longer perform as expected. The implications for portfolio construction are significant and underappreciated.

"The danger is not that inflation returns with a vengeance. The danger is that it remains structurally elevated at a level that reprices every financial asset modeled on a 2% assumption."

Leading Indicators

Three signals deserve close attention. First, the labor market continues to demonstrate structural rigidity inconsistent with prior late-cycle readings. Prime-age participation has recovered to pre-pandemic levels, yet wage growth in services — the stickiest component — remains above levels consistent with the Fed's 2% inflation target.

Second, the housing market's lagged contribution to CPI has distorted headline readings in ways that will gradually normalize over the next twelve months, potentially revealing underlying core inflation that has not meaningfully declined. The market has repeatedly front-run disinflation only to encounter data surprises to the upside.

Third — and most underappreciated — is the fiscal backdrop. The U.S. is running a deficit exceeding 6% of GDP in a non-recessionary environment. Historically, this scale of fiscal impulse has not coexisted with persistent disinflation without either (a) monetary tightening that induces recession or (b) financial repression that holds real rates artificially low.

Asset Class Implications

If the disinflation regime is genuinely exhausted, the most immediate re-pricing occurs in long-duration nominal bonds. The conventional wisdom that Treasuries serve as reliable equity hedges depends critically on a negative stock-bond correlation — a relationship that holds only when inflation is low and central banks are primarily responsive to growth shocks rather than supply-driven price shocks.

"A world where bonds and equities sell off together is not a theoretical curiosity — it is the dominant regime across most of financial history."

Real assets — commodities, infrastructure, TIPS, and select real estate subsectors — have historically served as more robust stores of value in such environments. The challenge for most institutional portfolios is that allocations to these asset classes were dramatically reduced during the decade of suppressed inflation, creating structural underweighting precisely when the regime argues for overweighting.

Positioning Framework

We do not advocate for wholesale repositioning based on any single regime hypothesis. Rather, we believe the evidence warrants a re-examination of implicit assumptions embedded in current portfolios — particularly the assumption that duration will reliably hedge equity drawdowns.

A barbell approach — combining short-duration credit with inflation-sensitive real assets — offers a more robust risk profile across the range of plausible macro outcomes than a traditional 60/40 allocation that depends on the continuation of a regime that may no longer obtain.
