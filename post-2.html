Credit Spreads as a Regime Signal: A Historical Audit

January 2025 Elysian Capital Research ~10 min read

Introduction

Credit spreads occupy a peculiar position in the macro investor's toolkit. They are simultaneously a coincident indicator — reflecting current risk appetite — and a leading indicator of the economic cycle, because the tightening of financial conditions that spreads signal often precedes the real-economy consequences by six to eighteen months.

This essay presents an audit of investment-grade and high-yield spread behavior across the major U.S. economic cycles since 1990, with particular attention to the signal reliability at cycle inflection points and the integration of spread dynamics into a regime-aware allocation model.

"Spread compression to historic lows is not a signal to buy credit — it is a signal that the market is pricing out the future that credit investors most need to prepare for."

Historical Pattern

Across the cycles examined — 1990-91, 2001, 2007-09, and 2020 — high-yield spreads provided a meaningful early warning signal in each instance. In the 2007 cycle, HY spreads began widening in June 2007, more than twelve months before the recession's trough. In 2001, the widening began in late 1999, nearly eighteen months before the formal contraction.

The 2020 cycle is instructive for different reasons: spread widening was simultaneous rather than leading, reflecting the exogenous shock nature of the pandemic. This suggests that spread signals are most reliable when the economic deterioration is endogenous — driven by financial imbalances rather than external shocks.

The current environment, with spreads near cyclical lows across both IG and HY, is historically consistent with late-cycle rather than mid-cycle positioning. This does not predict imminent deterioration, but it does imply limited asymmetric upside to adding credit risk.

Signal Construction

The signal framework we employ uses three inputs: (1) the absolute level of HY spreads relative to the trailing 10-year percentile; (2) the rate of change of spreads over the prior 13 and 26 weeks; and (3) the spread between investment-grade and high-yield as a measure of within-credit risk differentiation.

"The most dangerous moment in credit is not when spreads are wide — it is when they narrow past the point of memory and investors begin treating compression as a new equilibrium."

When all three components align — low absolute spreads, momentum decelerating, and IG/HY differential compressing — the signal argues for reducing credit exposure and extending duration in higher-quality instruments. This combination has historically occurred between 8 and 18 months before cycle peaks.

Allocation Implications

For practitioners, the framework translates to a simple heuristic: spread-aware credit allocation should rotate dynamically between (a) opportunistic HY exposure when spreads are in the top quartile of historical distribution and (b) IG and Treasury duration when spreads are compressed to cyclically low levels.

The challenge is behavioral rather than analytical. In a spread-compression environment, the carry return on credit creates short-term performance pressure against the signal. Most institutional mandates do not tolerate the tracking error required to act on the signal early. This is precisely why the signal has persistent predictive value — it is structurally difficult to act on.
